Self-Replicating AI Worm Runs Entirely on Local Open-Weight Models

Read the article: The Hacker News

University of Toronto researchers have built an AI-powered computer worm that can break into networks on its own, no human guidance required. In lab tests on a 33-machine network, the worm found vulnerabilities, gained access to systems, and spread itself to 62 percent of the network within seven days.

Unlike traditional malware, which relies on a fixed set of pre-programmed attack methods, this worm thinks on the fly. It reads the latest public security advisories and adapts its approach to whatever it finds on each new target. That means patching a single known flaw is not enough to stop it.

What makes it especially difficult to contain is that it runs on freely available AI models, with no connection to commercial AI providers. There is no account to suspend, no API key to cut off. The researchers have not released the code publicly, but they argue the findings should push organizations to rethink how quickly they respond to newly disclosed vulnerabilities, because the window between a flaw being published and it being exploited may now be measured in hours.

Responsible AI

Stanford Study Finds AI Hiring Tools Disadvantage Black and Asian Applicants

Read the article: The Root

Algorithmic hiring tools, now used by more than 90 percent of companies worldwide for initial candidate screening, may be amplifying racial discrimination at a scale that individual rejections never could. That is the central finding of a new study from Stanford University's Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence institute, which the researchers describe as the first large-scale analysis of hiring algorithms operating in real-world conditions.

The team examined more than 4 million job applications from 3.4 million people across 156 employers using the same AI hiring vendor. They found the tools effectively locked out nearly 26 percent of Black candidates and also produced negative effects for Asian applicants. Had these groups been recommended at the same rate as the most-favored group, typically white applicants, 40,000 more applications would have advanced. The study also identified offsetting patterns, with Black candidates less likely to be recommended in finance but more likely in warehouse roles, which can make discrimination appear absent.

The researchers tied these outcomes to market concentration and warned that widespread use, high-stakes consequences, and limited transparency rarely belong together. They call for independent research to support evidence-based AI policy and accountability.

China Launches World's First Wind-Powered Underwater Datacentre off Shanghai

Read the article: The Guardian - Technology

China has launched the world's first wind-powered underwater datacentre off the coast of Shanghai, advancing the country's efforts to address energy challenges driven by its artificial intelligence expansion. The Shanghai Lingang undersea project, which began operations in May, has a capacity of 24 megawatts and is a joint effort between HiCloud Technology and the state-owned China Communications Construction.

Submerged 10 metres below the surface more than 6 miles offshore, the facility draws power from a nearby offshore windfarm. The Chinese government reports it reduces power consumption by over one-fifth compared with land-based datacentres, partly because seawater provides natural cooling. Traditional facilities can spend 25% to 40% of their electricity on chilling servers and have faced scrutiny over their freshwater use. The United Nations University recently warned that datacentre water consumption could reach 9.3tn litres by 2030.

The project, which received 1.6bn yuan in investment, builds on HiCloud's 2023 commercial underwater datacentre in Hainan and follows an earlier Microsoft pilot in Scotland. Experts noted potential risks to marine ecosystems, including disturbed sediment and warmer seawater
