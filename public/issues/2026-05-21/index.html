Anthropic Pays SpaceX $15 Billion Annually for AI Computing Infrastructure

Read the article: WIRED

Anthropic has agreed to pay SpaceX $1.25 billion per month — roughly $15 billion annually — for access to GPU computing infrastructure at Colossus and Colossus II, a pair of data centers in Tennessee and Mississippi with more than one gigawatt of computing power. The arrangement, which runs through May 2029, was disclosed in SpaceX's S-1 IPO filing released Wednesday. Notably, the deal sends Anthropic's money to a direct competitor: SpaceX's xAI unit develops the Grok AI chatbot. An Anthropic spokesperson confirmed the figures to WIRED.

The disclosure underscores how compute access has become a critical bottleneck in AI development. Anthropic's quarterly revenue is expected to exceed $10 billion, according to the Wall Street Journal, suggesting the company views the steep infrastructure cost as necessary to sustain its growth. SpaceX, meanwhile, describes a "dual monetization strategy" in which it continues using the data centers for its own AI models while leasing excess capacity to outside parties — and says it expects to sign additional similar agreements.

The S-1 filing, part of what could be the largest IPO in U.S. history targeting a $1.75 trillion valuation, also drew scrutiny over governance provisions that give Elon Musk near-unchecked control over SpaceX's board and leadership structure.

Sam Altman Offers YC Startups $2M in OpenAI Credits for Equity Stakes

Read the article: TechCrunch

Sam Altman made a notable offer at a Y Combinator event this week: $2 million worth of OpenAI API tokens to each startup in YC's current cohort of approximately 169 companies, in exchange for equity. The deal would be structured as an uncapped SAFE, converting at the startup's first priced round — typically a Series A — meaning the equity stake OpenAI ultimately receives depends on that future valuation.

The arrangement serves OpenAI on two fronts: it acquires equity positions across a broad portfolio of early-stage companies and steers those startups toward building on OpenAI's infrastructure rather than competitors' platforms. Critics, including seed investor Jason Calacanis, have raised concerns that OpenAI could study and replicate startup ideas — though the article notes Altman already has extensive access to YC cohorts regardless of any investment deal.

For founders, the calculus involves weighing reduced AI infrastructure costs against surrendering additional equity on top of YC's standard 7% stake. The risk is straightforward: a startup could exhaust its token allocation without meaningful traction, having given up equity for a resource that may depreciate in value as inference costs continue to fall.

The deal reflects broader momentum in defense tech investment. For legal professionals and law students exploring AI-assisted practice, the project offers a structured, ready-to-deploy starting point with significant customization options.
