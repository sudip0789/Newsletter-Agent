Pentagon clears 8 tech firms to deploy their AI on its classified networks

Read the article: Freerepublic.com

The Pentagon has authorized eight major technology companies to deploy artificial intelligence systems on its classified networks, marking a significant expansion of commercial AI into military operations. The approved companies include Amazon Web Services, Google, Microsoft, OpenAI, SpaceX, NVIDIA, Reflection, and Oracle, with some already under contract while others finalize agreement details.

The AI systems will operate on networks classified as Impact Level 6 (handling secret data) and Impact Level 7 (the most highly classified systems). According to the Pentagon, these agreements support Secretary Pete Hegseth's initiative to transform the U.S. military into an "AI-first fighting force" and enhance warfighters' decision-making capabilities across all warfare domains.

This development builds on the December launch of the GenAI.mil platform for secure but unclassified operations. The Pentagon states the partnerships will accelerate military transformation and strengthen decision superiority, representing the most significant integration of commercial AI technology into classified defense networks to date.

Scott Turow's latest real-life legal thriller: Suing Meta for copyright infringement

Read the article: NPR

Major publishers Hachette, Macmillan, McGraw Hill, Elsevier and Cengage have joined bestselling author Scott Turow in a class-action lawsuit against Meta and CEO Mark Zuckerberg. Filed Tuesday in the Southern District of New York, the complaint alleges Meta knowingly copied copyrighted materials from pirate websites including LibGen and Anna's Archive to train its Llama language models, with Zuckerberg's personal authorization.

The lawsuit claims Meta initially considered licensing deals with publishers in April 2023 but abandoned those efforts after the decision was escalated to Zuckerberg. According to the complaint, Meta's business development team received verbal instructions to stop licensing, with one employee quoted as saying licensing would undermine their "fair use strategy." The plaintiffs cite specific stolen works including Turow's "Presumed Innocent" and books by Douglas Preston, N.K. Jemisin, and others.

The case represents a potentially massive class of copyright holders for books with ISBN numbers and journal articles with DOI or ISSN identifiers. Plaintiffs seek statutory damages, a permanent injunction, and destruction of all infringing materials. Meta plans to "fight this lawsuit aggressively," citing fair use protections, though the legal landscape remains mixed following Anthropic's recent $1.5 billion settlement with authors.

Anthropic owes authors $1.5B for pirating work — but the claims process is a Kafkaesque mess

Read the article: Vox

A federal judge approved a $1.5 billion settlement between AI company Anthropic and nearly half a million authors whose copyrighted books were downloaded from pirated databases to train the company's AI models without permission. The settlement, reached in September 2025 in Bartz et al. v. Anthropic PBC, marked the first of its kind in the AI industry, with similar lawsuits pending against Meta and OpenAI.

However, authors attempting to claim their portion of the settlement have encountered significant technical difficulties with the claims administration website. The system has proven glitchy and unreliable, forcing authors to repeatedly submit paperwork and navigate bureaucratic obstacles. The underlying data used to identify eligible authors came from the original pirate library metadata, which lawyers acknowledge is incomplete and inaccurate due to the rushed timeline imposed by the court.

While the $1.5 billion settlement initially appears substantial, individual payouts are modest—estimated at around $3,000 per book, split between authors and publishers. For many writers, this amounts to hundreds rather than thousands of dollars per work. The settlement faces a fairness hearing on May 14, where the judge will review author complaints about inadequate compensation relative to the alleged damage to their careers and the publishing industry.

Apple Will Pay $250 Million to Settle Lawsuit Over Siri’s AI Features

Read the article: WIRED

Apple has agreed to pay $250 million to settle a class-action lawsuit alleging the company engaged in false advertising about its Apple Intelligence features, particularly promised AI enhancements to Siri. The lawsuit claims Apple saturated the market with deceptive ads that induced consumers to purchase iPhones based on enhanced Siri capabilities first announced at the 2024 Worldwide Developers Conference, ahead of the iPhone 16 release.

The settlement covers US customers who bought iPhone 15 or iPhone 16 models between June 2024 and March 2025, with eligible claimants potentially receiving $25 to $95 per device from a $250 million fund. Court documents reveal that the Better Business Bureau's National Advertising Division found Apple's claim that Apple Intelligence was "available now" misleadingly suggested the updated Siri was available at launch when it was not. Apple acknowledged in March 2025 that enhanced Siri features would not be delivered until a future date.

This marks one of Apple's largest settlements and the second costly Siri-related legal resolution in two years, following a $95 million settlement in 2025 over privacy concerns. The settlement awaits judicial approval and includes no admission of fault by Apple.

Accelerating Gemma 4: faster inference with multi-token prediction drafters

Read the article: Google - The Keyword

Google has released Multi-Token Prediction (MTP) drafters for its Gemma 4 open-source AI model family, delivering up to 3x faster inference speeds without compromising output quality. The technology uses speculative decoding, where a lightweight drafter model predicts multiple tokens simultaneously while a larger target model verifies them in parallel, addressing the memory-bandwidth bottleneck that typically limits standard language model inference.

The MTP drafters are designed to benefit developers across deployment scenarios, from real-time chat applications and coding assistants to on-device mobile apps. Google reports that the technique allows complex models like the 26B mixture-of-experts and 31B dense versions to run more efficiently on consumer hardware, while edge models like E2B and E4B can preserve battery life through faster generation.

The drafters are available under the Apache 2.0 license and can be accessed through Hugging Face, Kaggle, and various inference frameworks including transformers, MLX, VLLM, and Ollama. Google has also published technical documentation detailing the architectural enhancements, including KV cache sharing and efficient clustering techniques that enable the performance gains.

Amazon Web Services has launched a preview service that allows AI agents to operate virtual desktop environments through its WorkSpaces platform. Agents receive unique identities via Amazon's Identity and Access Management service and access WorkSpaces through pre-signed URLs, where they can control applications using computer vision, screenshots, mouse control, and text input. AWS recommends giving each agent a distinct identity to track activities and distinguish them from human users.

The service targets organizations seeking automated software operations in isolated cloud environments rather than on local networks. However, cost concerns may limit adoption. While improved AI models may reduce costs over time, Reflex argues that agents will inherently require more steps than direct API calls to complete tasks.

Security

Red-teaming a network of agents: Understanding what breaks when AI agents interact at scale

Read the article: Microsoft Research Blog

Microsoft researchers red-teamed a live internal platform with over 100 AI agents to identify network-level security vulnerabilities that don't appear when testing agents individually. The study revealed four distinct attack patterns that emerge only when agents interact at scale, including self-propagating "worms" that spread malicious instructions from agent to agent while collecting private data at each step.

In one experiment, a single malicious message framed as a relay game caused agents to access their principals' private wallet data and forward attack instructions to other agents. The attack reached all six test agents and continued circulating for over twelve minutes, creating both a data breach and denial-of-service condition that consumed over 100 LLM calls billed to victims. Other observed risks included reputation manipulation where attackers used trusted agents to spread false claims, and "trust capture" attacks that corrupted verification systems.

The research highlights critical security gaps as AI agent networks become more common across platforms like email, GitHub, and various AI assistants. While researchers identified some early defensive behaviors among agents, the findings suggest that traditional single-agent security testing misses significant vulnerabilities that only manifest through multi-agent interactions.

Google Fixes CVSS 10 Gemini CLI CI RCE and Cursor Flaws Enable Code Execution

Read the article: The Hacker News

Google has patched a maximum severity vulnerability (CVSS 10.0) in its Gemini CLI tool that allowed attackers to execute arbitrary commands on host systems. The flaw affected the "@google/gemini-cli" npm package and "google-github-actions/run-gemini-cli" GitHub Actions workflow, enabling unprivileged external attackers to force malicious content to load as Gemini configuration and trigger command execution directly on host systems.

The vulnerability stemmed from Gemini CLI automatically trusting workspace folders when running in headless mode for CI environments, allowing the tool to load agent configurations without review or user consent. Attackers could exploit this by planting specially crafted configurations in untrusted directories, effectively turning CI/CD pipelines into supply-chain attack vectors. The issue particularly affected workflows reviewing user-submitted pull requests or processing other untrusted inputs.

Google's fix requires folders to be explicitly trusted before configuration files can be accessed. Users must now either set "GEMINI_TRUST_WORKSPACE: 'true'" for trusted workflows or follow Google's hardening guidance for untrusted inputs. Separately, security researchers disclosed vulnerabilities in the AI development tool Cursor, including a high-severity sandbox escape flaw (CVE-2026-26268) that enables code execution through malicious Git hooks and an unpatched access control issue allowing extensions to steal API keys from local databases.

Doctors' growing AI deepfakes problem

Read the article: Axios

The American Medical Association is calling for new federal and state legislation to combat deepfake videos that impersonate doctors to promote questionable medical products and spread misinformation. AMA CEO John Whyte characterized the problem as a public health and safety crisis, noting that physicians increasingly discover their identities used to hawk wellness supplements and unapproved medical devices. Even CNN's Sanjay Gupta reported that deepfakes using his likeness have become so convincing they've fooled his acquaintances.

The threat extends beyond individual doctors to healthcare systems themselves. A recent study in Radiology found that most clinicians failed to identify deepfake X-rays, with one-quarter missing the fakes even after being warned about telltale signs. Researchers warn these synthetic medical images could enable insurance fraud or, if injected into hospital networks by hackers, manipulate patient diagnoses and create clinical chaos.

California has begun requiring disclosures on AI-generated advertisements and is considering legislation specifically banning doctor deepfakes. Pennsylvania's medical board recently ordered a tech company to stop its chatbot from impersonating a licensed physician. The AMA seeks guidance on how targeted doctors should respond and how malpractice insurance might address potential liability when patients are harmed by advice the real physician never gave.

Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei warned of a critical cybersecurity window during a discussion with JPMorgan Chase CEO Jamie Dimon, revealing that the company's Mythos AI model has uncovered tens of thousands of software vulnerabilities across widely used programs. The warnings have sparked industry debate, with OpenAI CEO Sam Altman suggesting Anthropic may be using "fear-based marketing" to justify limiting access to the technology. Despite this criticism, the U.S. government is reportedly using Claude Mythos to scan classified networks for vulnerabilities, according to Axios.

India orders infosec red alert in case Mythos sparks crime spree

Read the article: The Register

India's Securities and Exchange Board has issued an urgent advisory directing financial market participants to immediately strengthen their cybersecurity defenses against potential threats from Anthropic's Mythos AI bug-finding system. The regulator warned that the AI tool could enable a surge in cyberattacks and established a taskforce to examine risks, share threat intelligence, and review cybersecurity practices among third-party software vendors.

The advisory targets 19 different types of financial entities, from venture capitalists to stock exchanges, ordering them to update patches, audit vulnerabilities, secure APIs, and implement zero-trust networking principles. Companies must also develop AI-augmented security operations and create mitigation plans for AI-detected vulnerabilities.

India's response represents one of the most proactive regulatory approaches globally to the Mythos threat. While other regulators including the US Treasury, Singapore, Australia, and Hong Kong have acknowledged similar risks, India stands out for putting its financial sector on immediate alert and mandating specific defensive actions rather than merely issuing guidance.

New frontier of AI forces Trump's heavy hand

Read the article: Axios

President Trump's administration is preparing to establish federal oversight of powerful AI models, marking a dramatic reversal from its initial deregulatory approach. The White House is weighing an executive order that would create a formal government role in vetting new AI models before market release, including a working group of tech executives and officials to design the oversight process. The administration is also developing an AI security framework requiring Pentagon safety testing of models used by government agencies.

The policy shift follows the emergence of extraordinarily capable AI models like Anthropic's Mythos and OpenAI's GPT-5.5, which can identify cybersecurity vulnerabilities with unprecedented speed and precision. These capabilities have crossed a threshold that even an ideologically deregulatory administration cannot ignore, particularly as Chinese labs race to develop similar technology.

Top AI companies are cooperating with the new oversight efforts, recognizing the need to partner with government to avoid more restrictive measures. The Department of Commerce announced new testing agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft and xAI, while sources suggest a formal agreement could emerge within weeks. This represents a stark contrast to Trump's first-day rescission of Biden's AI executive order and the administration's systematic dismantling of previous safety measures.

Higher Education

Using AI for Just 10 Minutes Might Make You Lazy and Dumb, Study Shows

Read the article: WIRED

Researchers from Carnegie Mellon, MIT, Oxford, and UCLA found that using AI chatbots for just 10 minutes can significantly impair people's problem-solving abilities. In three experiments involving several hundred participants each, those who used AI assistants to solve problems like fractions and reading comprehension were more likely to give up or provide incorrect answers when the AI was removed. The study suggests AI use may boost immediate productivity while undermining the development of foundational cognitive skills.

MIT's Michiel Bakker, a co-author of the study, emphasizes that persistence in problem-solving is crucial for acquiring new skills and predicts long-term learning capacity. The research indicates that AI systems providing direct answers may have different effects than those that scaffold or coach users. Bakker suggests rethinking AI tool design so they sometimes prioritize user learning over immediate problem resolution, similar to how effective human teachers operate.

The findings raise concerns for legal education and practice, where critical thinking and analytical skills are fundamental. As AI tools become more prevalent in legal research and drafting, the study suggests careful consideration of how these technologies are integrated to preserve essential cognitive abilities.
