Customers include Mercedes-Benz, NASA, Goldman Sachs, and Santander. Legal Intelligence

Court Allows Disney Copyright Suit Against Chinese AI Firm MiniMax to Proceed

Read the article: The Times of India

A federal judge in California has allowed a major copyright lawsuit against Chinese AI company MiniMax to move forward. US District Judge Stanley Blumenfeld rejected MiniMax's motion to dismiss, finding that the court has jurisdiction over the company and that the studios' complaint states plausible claims for copyright infringement.

Walt Disney, Comcast's Universal, and Warner Bros Discovery sued MiniMax last year, alleging the company trained its Hailuo image-and-video-generating AI system on their copyrighted content without permission. The studios further alleged that MiniMax used characters from the Marvel universe, Star Wars, and other franchises to market Hailuo as a "Hollywood studio in your pocket." MiniMax had argued the California court lacked jurisdiction because it conducts its business in China, but Judge Blumenfeld found evidence that the Hailuo app was being offered to US users.

The ruling is a significant procedural development in the broader wave of AI copyright litigation. The same studios have filed parallel suits against AI company Midjourney, and the case joins a growing body of litigation from media companies, authors, and news organizations challenging the use of their content to train AI systems.

Harvey Releases Open-Source Benchmark to Evaluate AI Agents on Complex Legal Tasks

Read the article: Lawnext.com

Harvey, the legal AI company valued at $11 billion, released the Legal Agent Benchmark (LAB) on May 6, an open-source evaluation framework designed to measure how well AI agents perform extended legal work. Unlike existing benchmarks such as LegalBench and CUAD, which test discrete reasoning tasks, LAB focuses on longer-horizon assignments modeled on partner-to-associate delegations. The initial release includes more than 1,200 tasks across 24 practice areas, graded against over 75,000 expert-written rubric criteria. Notably, Harvey launched without a leaderboard, stating it will collaborate with research partners to establish baseline results and submission standards before publishing rankings.

LAB uses "all-pass" grading, meaning a task is complete only if every rubric criterion is met, reflecting the reality that missing a single material issue in a deal memo can have serious consequences. Harvey acknowledges contributions from major AI labs including Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google DeepMind. For law firms, the benchmark could provide a shared framework for vendor evaluation beyond demo-driven procurement decisions.

The article raises a significant caveat: LAB was built by a commercial market participant, meaning Harvey's team shaped the definitions of acceptable legal work product. Questions also remain about whether the project will attract genuine outside contributors or function primarily as a vendor showcase.

Musk vs. OpenAI Trial Ends Without Verdict, But Reveals Profit Pressures

Read the article: Japan Today

A three-week federal trial in Oakland pitting Elon Musk against OpenAI and CEO Sam Altman ended Monday without a verdict on the merits. The jury dismissed Musk's lawsuit on procedural grounds, finding it missed a statutory deadline. Musk had accused Altman and co-founder Greg Brockman of betraying OpenAI's original nonprofit mission and unjustly enriching themselves; OpenAI countered that Musk had supported the for-profit conversion and filed suit only to disadvantage a competitor as he built his own AI company, xAI.

Internal emails and witness accounts, including testimony from Microsoft CTO Kevin Scott and former chief scientist Ilya Sutskever, documented how the capital demands of large-scale AI development left the organization with few alternatives to commercial investment. For legal professionals tracking AI governance and fiduciary obligations in emerging-technology organizations, the trial record offers a rare window into how structural decisions at a major AI company were actually made.

Security researchers warn that patches or mitigations are urgently needed given the scale of exposure.

Higher Education

UC Berkeley Law Tightens AI Rules to Protect Core Legal Skills

Read the article: Business Insider

UC Berkeley Law School is tightening its AI policy this summer, moving well beyond its 2023 rules, which faculty now consider too permissive. The updated policy prohibits students from using AI for conceptualizing, outlining, drafting, revising, editing, translating, or in any exam context. The 2023 policy had allowed brainstorming and conceptualization with AI tools.

Professor Chris Hoofnagle, who helped draft the new rules, frames the policy as a response to the rapid advancement of large language models. His concern is less about AI itself and more about what happens when students rely on it before mastering foundational legal skills. "If that lawyer cannot use their own analytical judgment to assess an AI output, that lawyer has very little value," he told Business Insider.

The policy comes with acknowledged limits. Hoofnagle notes that tools like Westlaw and Lexis now surface AI-generated summaries by default, making a clean line between permitted and prohibited use difficult to draw. Berkeley has also seen an increase in academic misconduct cases and has shifted more exams to in-person, proctored formats as a result.

Teachers Are Using AI at Work but Receiving Almost No Formal Guidance

Read the article: Axios

A new report from Gallup and the Walton Family Foundation finds that while K-12 teachers are already using AI on the job, roughly eight in ten report receiving no formal guidance from school leaders on how to apply the tools. Gaps extend across specific use cases: around 71% received no guidance on using AI for feedback or coaching on their teaching, and 69% received none on one-on-one instruction or tutoring. Teachers in higher-needs schools were less likely to receive guidance than those in wealthier institutions.

Education experts and school administrators quoted in the report warn that the absence of structured support is pushing teachers to integrate AI in piecemeal ways that may not improve student outcomes. The CEO of All4Ed notes that the question is no longer whether students will use AI, but whether educators have the professional development and institutional backing to use it confidently. One ed-tech founder cautions that AI "can amplify the best parts of teaching and the worst," underscoring that volume of adoption matters less than strategic implementation.
